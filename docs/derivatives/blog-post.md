# The Forecast Your Company Negotiates Is the Forecast You Can't Defend

It's Monday at 9 a.m. Four functions show up to a recurring meeting with four different versions of next quarter's forecast. Sales has one number — built on the deals their reps think they'll close. Operations has another — built on what the plants can actually ship. Finance has a third — built on what the board has been told. Channel partners aren't in the meeting; nobody's quite sure what their demand signal is right now.

By Friday, a number has been reconciled. It lands in the board pack. It is, technically, the company's forecast.

It is also a negotiated artifact, not a computed one. And that is the structural problem most mid-market commercial companies have not solved.

## Why Forecasts Get Negotiated Instead of Computed

The negotiation isn't a failure of people. Each function has rational, defensible reasons to see the future the way they see it. Sales is paid on closed business and tends to be optimistic. Operations is paid on delivery and tends to be conservative. Finance is paid on hitting the number and tends to be neither — depending on whether the conversation is internal or external.

The negotiation is a failure of architecture. The data each function uses to build its number lives in a different system, gets pulled at a different time, and is filtered through that function's worldview before it lands in a workbook. There is no shared substrate — no single artifact every function is editing. So the only reconciliation mechanism available is conversation.

And conversation can do many things, but it cannot produce a defensible number.

## The Cost Shows Up Downstream

A negotiated forecast is expensive in ways that don't show up on the forecast itself. They show up on the next four quarters of operating reality.

Inventory positioning gets misaligned with demand because operations is planning against a different version than what sales committed to. Some SKUs overstock; others run out. Customer commitments get made that operations can't deliver, which becomes either lost revenue or eroded relationship — sometimes both. Working capital ties up in inventory you didn't need to hold. Markdown rate climbs because you're aging product nobody planned for.

When the market shifts inside a quarter — a customer accelerates, a tariff changes, a competitor moves — you can't re-run the forecast. Not because the math is hard, but because the negotiation has to happen again. So the response window is measured in weeks while the market window is measured in days.

And when leadership asks the question that always gets asked — "how did we arrive at this number?" — there is no audit trail. There's a meeting that happened. There are four spreadsheets. There is no defensible answer.

## Start With the Metric You're On the Hook to Move

The fix doesn't start with a tool. It starts with a question we ask before any technology decision: what is the specific number you're accountable for moving this fiscal year?

For some operations leaders, it's forecast accuracy itself — moving from sixty-something percent into the high eighties. For others, it's working capital tied up in inventory, measured in dollars released. For others, it's customer-commitment delivery rate, or days-to-revise-forecast, or stockout rate by SKU class.

The number doesn't have to be small. It has to be specific, owned, and defensible to the board. If three executives in the room would name three different numbers, the engagement doesn't start until that's resolved. Strategy first means agreeing on the metric before agreeing on the means.

## Architect the Forecast — It's Not a Configuration Task

The conventional move at this point is to evaluate forecasting tools. We do something different. We architect the forecast itself before we touch the technology.

That means sitting with operations, sales, finance, and (when relevant) channel partners and answering a sequence of strategic questions. What dimensions does your business actually plan against — product hierarchies, ship-from locations, territories, business units, customer segments? Which combinations of those matter for which decisions? What measures are you tracking — quantity, revenue, margin, commitment shortfall, adjustment delta? Who owns adjustments to which measures, during which time windows? How does the operating cadence — sales submissions, operations review, channel-partner input — overlay on those windows?

These aren't configuration decisions. They are the company's working definition of how it sees its own future. Most companies have never written that definition down. The work of writing it down is, by itself, often the single highest-leverage thing we do in an engagement — long before any system changes.

## The Smallest Change That Moves the Number

Once the forecast is architected, the technology question becomes much smaller and much sharper. Sometimes the right answer is standing up a new forecasting capability inside the system the customer already runs. Sometimes it's integrating signal feeds between the planning system and the customer system that holds the relationship data. Sometimes it's neither — the operating model needs to be fixed first, and any technology change before that fix would just digitize a broken process.

We pick the path with the largest outcome and the smallest surface area. And we tell the customer when the answer is "not yet" rather than "more software."

## Tie the SOW to the Outcome

This is the part that most differentiates a strategy engagement from a typical implementation engagement — and it's the test we apply to ourselves before we sign.

The Statement of Work closes on the business outcome we committed to moving — forecast-accuracy delta, working-capital release, stockout reduction. Not on go-live. Not on adoption rate. Not on training completion. The system isn't the deliverable; the change in the number is the deliverable.

That alignment changes how the work gets done. It changes who staffs the engagement. It changes what gets escalated and what gets absorbed. It changes how we know when we're finished.

A negotiated forecast can't tell you what to do. A computed one can. The companies that build the second kind first don't just forecast better — they ship inventory better, defend their numbers better, and respond to demand shifts faster. The first question is always the same: what's the specific number you're on the hook to move?

Everything else follows.
