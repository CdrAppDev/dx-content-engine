Your forecast is a number. The thing your company calls "the forecast" is something else.

It's a negotiation. Four spreadsheets, four function owners, a Monday meeting, a managed compromise that lands in the board pack on Friday.

Your operations team plans against one version. Your sales team commits to another. Your channel partners aren't in either.

When the market shifts mid-quarter, you can't re-run it. When leadership asks how it was built, there's no audit trail.

The fix isn't a smarter spreadsheet or another dashboard. It's a different question — what would it look like if every function adjusted the same forecast during their own role-defined window, and the system did the reconciliation?

Monday meetings drop to fifteen minutes. The audit trail is automatic. Forecast revisions happen in hours, not days. Channel partners contribute their demand signal directly — for the first time.

A negotiated forecast can't tell you what to do. A computed one can.

The first question isn't "what tool?" — it's "what's the specific number you're on the hook to move?"

Everything else follows.
