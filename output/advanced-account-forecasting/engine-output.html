The Shift

Blog post The Forecast Your Company Negotiates Is the Forecast You Can’t Defend
~1,050 words · SEO + long-tail

It’s Monday at 9 a.m. Four functions show up to a recurring meeting with four different versions of next quarter’s forecast. Sales has one number — built on the deals their reps think they’ll close. Operations has another — built on what the plants can actually ship. Finance has a third — built on what the board has been told. Channel partners aren’t in the meeting; nobody’s quite sure what their demand signal is right now.

By Friday, a number has been reconciled. It lands in the board pack. It is, technically, the company’s forecast.

It is also a negotiated artifact, not a computed one. And that is the structural problem most mid-market commercial companies have not solved.

Why Forecasts Get Negotiated Instead of Computed

The negotiation isn’t a failure of people. Each function has rational, defensible reasons to see the future the way they see it. Sales is paid on closed business and tends to be optimistic. Operations is paid on delivery and tends to be conservative. Finance is paid on hitting the number and tends to be neither — depending on whether the conversation is internal or external.

The negotiation is a failure of architecture. The data each function uses to build its number lives in a different system, gets pulled at a different time, and is filtered through that function’s worldview before it lands in a workbook. There is no shared substrate — no single artifact every function is editing. So the only reconciliation mechanism available is conversation.

And conversation can do many things, but it cannot produce a defensible number.

The Cost Shows Up Downstream

A negotiated forecast is expensive in ways that don’t show up on the forecast itself. They show up on the next four quarters of operating reality. Inventory positioning gets misaligned. Some SKUs overstock; others run out. Customer commitments get made that operations can’t deliver. Working capital ties up in inventory you didn’t need to hold.

When the market shifts inside a quarter, you can’t re-run the forecast. Not because the math is hard, but because the negotiation has to happen again. So the response window is measured in weeks while the market window is measured in days.

Start with the Metric You’re on the Hook to Move

The fix doesn’t start with a tool. It starts with a question we ask before any technology decision: what is the specific number you’re accountable for moving this fiscal year? Forecast accuracy, working-capital release, customer-commitment delivery rate, days-to-revise-forecast. The number anchors everything that follows.

Tie the SOW to the Outcome

The Statement of Work closes on the business outcome we committed to moving — forecast-accuracy delta, working-capital release, stockout reduction. Not on go-live. The system isn’t the deliverable; the change in the number is the deliverable.

A negotiated forecast can’t tell you what to do. A computed one can.
