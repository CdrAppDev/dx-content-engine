# Theme

The overarching narrative DX Foundation is telling. Every content skill loads
this file as context.

## Big idea

**Strategy first. Technology second.** Most consultancies sell software implementations. We sell business outcomes — and we use software to deliver them. The technology is the means, not the message.

## Worldview

- Most enterprise software underdelivers because it gets bought before the business problem is clearly defined. The fix isn't a better implementation — it's better strategy upstream of the implementation.
- A platform feature is not a benefit. The same feature solves a $50K problem for one company and a $5M problem for another. The work is in finding the second company.
- Decision-makers buy outcomes, not capabilities. CFOs don't buy "automated workflow orchestration" — they buy "close the books two days faster."
- Vendor neutrality is a feature, not a flaw. We partner deeply with Salesforce, Snowflake, Databricks, AWS, Mulesoft, Tableau — but our recommendation is whatever moves the business, not whatever earns us partner credits.
- Mid-market companies get either nothing (consultancies too small to be useful, too generic to be insightful) or the Big Four's discount tier. There is a serious gap between those two extremes — that's where we live.

## What we're against

- **Feature-speak.** Marketing pages that describe what the software does instead of what it changes for the buyer.
- **Implementation-first framing.** "We implement Salesforce" sells a noun. "We help businesses perform better" sells the verb.
- **One-size-fits-all decks.** A discovery process that produces the same recommendation regardless of the company in the room is not strategy — it's a sales motion.
- **Tech-stack maximalism.** More tools is not the answer to most problems.

## Recurring motifs

- **Strategy first** — repeat this until it's boring; then repeat it again
- **Performance** — businesses "perform better" (not "transform," not "modernize")
- **Outcomes over outputs** — the success metric is never a launch date
- **Plain language** — if a CFO can't paraphrase it, it's not done
- The brand's signature **four-point sparkle (✦)** marks strategy moments — the "aha," not the "ta-da"
